%% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Private Set Intersection (PSI) enables two mutually distrusting parties to
compute the overlap of their datasets without revealing any non-matching
elements. Most high-performance PSI protocols rely on classical cryptographic
assumptions that would be vulnerable to large-scale quantum attacks, making
them a poor fit for sensitive data that must remain protected for many years.

Laconic PSI is attractive because it reduces communication and allows the
server to publish a reusable digest of its dataset. However, post-quantum
laconic PSI has remained largely theoretical, with no prior implementation or
experimental study. In this work, we present the first end-to-end
implementation and empirical evaluation of a Ring-LWE-based laconic PSI
protocol in Go under the semi-honest security model, using a
reduced-parameter instantiation ($D=256$, $\approx$140-bit classical /
70-bit quantum security).

Our results indicate that a reduced-parameter Ring-LWE laconic PSI implementation can support settings
where communication efficiency and lattice-based assumptions matter more than
very low latency. The main computational cost lies in server preprocessing
and the intersection phase, with ring arithmetic emerging as the primary
optimisation target. We also show that a sharded distributed deployment can
substantially reduce runtime at $D=256$, and we use the resulting
bottleneck analysis to identify the engineering work needed to scale toward
NIST PQC Level~1 parameters ($D=2048$).

To demonstrate the practical relevance of the protocol, we introduce
\textbf{FLARE}, a proof-of-concept sanctions-screening prototype. FLARE
shows that privacy-preserving compliance workflows based on lattice
cryptography can be prototyped on commodity infrastructure for
overnight or batch-processing scenarios.


\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =============================================================================

A European bank must screen thousands of customers daily against
government-mandated sanctions lists, yet sharing those customer identifiers
with an external screening provider violates GDPR's data-minimisation
principle~\cite{gdpr2016}. Anti-money laundering law demands screening; privacy law prohibits
disclosure. This tension appears broadly in regulated financial workflows where
compliance checks must be performed without unnecessary disclosure of customer
identifiers.

\emph{Private Set Intersection} (PSI) resolves this dilemma: two parties
compute the intersection of their datasets without revealing any non-matching
elements to the other side.

\subsection{The Quantum Threat to Long-Lived Data}

Classical PSI protocols instantiated with elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman or
OT-based techniques~\cite{kolesnikov2016kkrt} achieve excellent practical
performance, but their security rests on the elliptic-curve discrete
logarithm assumption, which Shor's algorithm breaks on a sufficiently large
quantum computer~\cite{shor1994}.

The critical threat is not immediate; it is temporal. The
\emph{``harvest now, decrypt later''} (HNDL) strategy, in which adversaries archive
encrypted data today for future quantum decryption, is well-documented in the
intelligence community~\cite{nsa_cnsa2022}. An attacker needs only to wait for
the hardware, not for a cryptographic breakthrough. Financial records and compliance logs can have long retention horizons;
data protected by classical PSI today may be decryptable before those
records lose operational or regulatory sensitivity. For this threat class, only protocols whose security reduces to
lattice problems believed resistant to quantum algorithms provide a credible
long-term guarantee.

\subsection{Laconic PSI and the Communication Advantage}

Beyond quantum resistance, regulated industries face a second, independent
challenge: \emph{communication scale}. In classical PSI, communication grows
as $O(n + m)$, where $m$ is the server dataset size. When a mid-sized bank
($n \approx 10^{4}$ customers) queries a global sanctions database
($m \approx 10^{6}$ entries), each daily screening round transfers data
proportional to the \emph{entire} sanctions list, creating a continuous, large-scale
privacy exposure.

D\"{o}ttling et al.~\cite{dottling2023laconic} introduced \emph{Laconic PSI}
(DKLLMR23), a theoretical construction from Ring-LWE achieving both plausible
post-quantum security and $O(n \log m)$ communication. The server compresses
its database into a reusable Merkle-root digest; clients query using
ciphertexts whose size depends only on $n$ and $\log m$, not on $m$ itself.
For sufficiently large server sets, this yields an order-of-magnitude
communication advantage over classical $O(n+m)$ PSI. The exact crossover
depends on ciphertext encoding constants, the server-side leaf-space size,
and whether the implementation uses single-candidate or 2-choice Cuckoo
assignment. Because our correctness-preserving Cuckoo mode sends two
ciphertexts per query, it increases the constant factor while preserving the
asymptotic $O(n\log M)$ profile; under the constants used in this work, the
crossover moves to the several-million-record range.

\textbf{The gap.} DKLLMR23 is a purely asymptotic construction. No
implementation, no benchmark, and no engineering analysis existed prior to
this work. The protocol's real-world feasibility, including its memory footprint,
throughput, latency, and deployment cost, was entirely unknown.

\subsection{Our Contributions}

We address this implementation gap with four concrete contributions.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

\item \textbf{First end-to-end implementation study.}
We implement DKLLMR23 end-to-end in Go using the Lattigo Ring-LWE library,
covering the full offline (server preprocessing) and online (client encryption
and server decryption) phases. The implementation achieves standard
semi-honest PSI security: both parties learn $C \cap S$; neither learns
non-matching elements of the other.

\item \textbf{Empirical characterisation of Ring-LWE laconic PSI costs.}
Through systematic single-node profiling ($m = 50$--$10{,}000$, $D=256$)
we establish the first concrete cost model.
Server initialisation ($\approx 29\%$) and per-record intersection
($\approx 69\%$) dominate wall-clock time. Within the intersection phase,
NTT ring operations ($\approx 28\%$ of CPU samples via \texttt{pprof})
are the dominant CPU consumer, indicating both memory- and compute-bound behavior. To enable
large-scale evaluation on standard HPC hardware we apply semaphore-limited
goroutine batching that bounds peak witness RAM to
$\Theta(W \cdot M_{\text{witness}})$ regardless of $m$.

\item \textbf{Distributed sharding architecture.}
Partitioning the server dataset across $K=7$ GCE nodes reduces
execution time from 153.1~minutes (single node) to 12.7~minutes
(${\approx}12.1\times$ measured speedup) under the full 2-choice Cuckoo
mode. Each shard operates as an
independent laconic PSI instance with its own Merkle digest; the coordinator
aggregates per-shard results.

\item \textbf{FLARE sanctions-screening prototype.}
We demonstrate FLARE, a proof-of-concept sanctions-screening system that
shows how Ring-LWE laconic PSI can support privacy-preserving compliance
workflows. We use FLARE to study deployment costs and identify the
remaining deployment, security, and parameter-scaling requirements for production use.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Performance Summary and Trade-offs}

Laconic PSI carries a substantial computational overhead relative to classical
protocols. At $m = 10{,}000$, KKRT~\cite{kolesnikov2016kkrt} completes in
under 0.3~seconds (from published benchmarks on different hardware); our
Ring-LWE protocol requires 153~minutes on a 96-core AMD EPYC~7763 with
77~workers. This overhead arises from the combination of Ring-LWE witness generation,
gadget decomposition, and the laconic per-leaf decryption structure.

We identify three deployment scenarios where this overhead may be justified
and the reusable-digest property provides a distinct advantage:
(1)~long-lived data for which production deployment would require stronger
parameters; (2)~highly unbalanced settings ($m \gg n$) where repeated
communication dominates; and (3)~overnight batch processing with relaxed
latency requirements. The FLARE prototype (Section~\ref{sec:flare})
exemplifies all three.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Classical PSI Protocols}

PSI has been studied extensively since Freedman et al.~\cite{freedman2004psi}.
OT-extension-based protocols represent the dominant practical paradigm.
KKRT~\cite{kolesnikov2016kkrt} introduced batched oblivious PRFs with Cuckoo
hashing, achieving roughly 200\,ms for $m = 4{,}096$ balanced pairs.
Raghuraman and Rindal~\cite{raghuraman2022blazing} improved upon KKRT using
Oblivious Key-Value Stores (OKVS), completing $n = 2^{20}$ items in
0.37~seconds. Circuit-PSI~\cite{pinkas2019efficient} enables computation
directly on the intersection using garbled circuits, trading communication
for expressiveness. All of these protocols achieve $O(n + m)$ communication.
While their base OT components can in principle be replaced with
post-quantum base-OT mechanisms to obtain post-quantum
security, no empirically validated post-quantum instantiation of
OT-extension PSI exists at comparable scale.

\subsection{Homomorphic Encryption PSI}

Chen et al.~\cite{chen2017fast} apply the BFV fully homomorphic encryption
scheme~\cite{fan2012fhe} to achieve $O(N_y \log N_x)$ communication for the
unbalanced setting ($N_x \gg N_y$), completing in approximately 36~seconds
(online) for $N_y = 5{,}000$ queries against $N_x = 2^{24}$ server records on
16~CPU threads. HE-PSI is conceptually related to laconic PSI in its sublinear
communication, but places the computational burden on the server rather than
producing the reusable-digest property of laconic encryption. A direct security-and-performance comparison with BFV-based PSI depends on
the concrete BFV parameters chosen for that application and is outside the
scope of this implementation study.


\subsection{Laconic PSI}

\emph{Laconic cryptography} refers to two-message protocols in which one
party commits to or compresses a large input so that subsequent communication
is sublinear in the size of that input. In the PSI setting, this means that
the server can publish a reusable digest of its set and clients can query that
digest using communication depending on the client set and the logarithm of
the server-side leaf space rather than on the full server set size.

Aranha et al.\ (ALOS22)~\cite{alos2022} provided the first empirically
validated laconic PSI protocol, constructed from pairings over BLS12-381 and
evaluated on an Intel Core i7-7820X. Their benchmarks covered equal-set sizes
up to $m = 2^{12} = 4{,}096$, with a server runtime of approximately
19~minutes at $m = 4{,}096$ over a 10\,Gbps LAN. ALOS22 is the closest
classical empirical baseline for our work: it provides a pairing-based
laconic PSI implementation, while we study the lattice-based Ring-LWE
analogue.

D\"{o}ttling et al.~\cite{dottling2023laconic} (DKLLMR23) introduced the
first laconic PSI construction from Ring-LWE, achieving plausible
post-quantum security at $O(n \log m)$ communication in the asymptotic
presentation. Their work is theoretical; no implementation, concrete
efficiency analysis, or benchmark was provided. The present paper addresses
this implementation and measurement gap.

\subsection{Post-Quantum PSI Alternatives}

Several post-quantum PSI constructions are emerging as practical alternatives.
VOLE-/OLE-based PSI~\cite{garimella2023okvs,raghuraman2022blazing} with
lattice-based VOLE instantiations achieve $O(n+m)$ communication with very
low online cost, at the expense of heavy offline precomputation. For
$m=10^{4}$, such constructions require approximately 2--10\,s online but
transfer $O(n+m)$ bytes. Under the concrete 2-choice Cuckoo
constants used in this work, laconic communication becomes advantageous only
in the several-million-record server-set regime.

Classical OT-extension PSI such as KKRT can be instantiated with
lattice-based OT (replacing Diffie-Hellman base OT with Ring-LWE OT),
preserving $O(n+m)$ communication at somewhat higher constant factors. No
empirically validated post-quantum OT-extension PSI at this scale has been
published at the time of writing; we compare against classical KKRT and
provide reasoned estimates for post-quantum variants in
Section~\ref{subsec:comparison}.

Our contribution is complementary to these efforts. The reusable-digest
property, where the offline phase is computed once and reused across multiple client rounds
with an $O(1)$ fresh-digest exchange per round, is unique to the laconic
paradigm and is not achievable with OT-extension PSI.

\subsection{Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardisation}

NIST has standardised Kyber (ML-KEM)~\cite{nist2022mlkem} and
Dilithium (ML-DSA)~\cite{nist2022mldsa} for key exchange and digital
signatures, respectively, demonstrating that lattice-based primitives are
practical for two-party protocols. Our work extends this evidence base to the
more complex setting of two-party computation, specifically PSI, where
Ring-LWE imposes a higher per-operation cost due to gadget decomposition.

\subsection{Ring Learning With Errors}
\label{subsec:rlwe}

\begin{definition}[Ring-LWE~\cite{lyubashevsky2010ring}]
Let $D$ be a power of~2, $q$ a prime modulus, and
$\Rq = \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^D + 1)$.
The Ring-LWE assumption states that, for a secret $s \in \Rq$ and error
distribution $\chi$ (a discrete Gaussian with standard deviation $\sigma$),
samples $(a_i,\, a_i s + e_i)$ with $a_i \xleftarrow{\$} \Rq$ and
$e_i \leftarrow \chi$ are computationally indistinguishable from uniformly
random.
\end{definition}

Security reduces to worst-case problems on ideal lattices~\cite{peikert2016decade},
believed intractable for quantum computers. Our implementation parameters are
detailed in Section~\ref{subsec:params}.

\paragraph{Choice of Ring-LWE}
Table~\ref{tab:lwe_variants} compares the principal lattice-based assumptions
considered for this work. Standard LWE~\cite{regev2009lattices} offers the strongest worst-case
reduction (to generic lattice problems), but its $O(n^2)$ key and ciphertext
sizes are prohibitive for laconic encryption, where every Merkle tree level
requires a full public key. Module-LWE (the basis of NIST's
ML-KEM/Kyber~\cite{nist2022mlkem}) offers a flexible security--efficiency
trade-off but has no published laconic PSI construction; adapting DKLLMR23 to
Module-LWE is non-trivial and would require new security proofs.

Ring-LWE occupies the optimal point for our setting: (i)~$O(D)$
public keys and $O(D \log D)$ polynomial arithmetic via the Number Theoretic
Transform (NTT); (ii)~native support for the GInvMNTT gadget decomposition
central to laconic decryption; (iii)~a mature, optimised implementation in
the Lattigo library~\cite{lattigo2024}; and (iv)~the DKLLMR23 construction
is formulated directly over Ring-LWE, providing a ready security proof.

\subsection{Merkle Trees}
\label{subsec:merkle}

Let $m = |S|$ denote the number of server records and let $M = 2^L$ denote
the Merkle leaf-space size used by the implementation. In the simplest
non-Cuckoo presentation one may take $M \approx m$; in the 2-choice Cuckoo
mode used in our benchmarks, records are assigned into a padded leaf space
with $M \geq m$ and empty leaves are filled with null public keys. We therefore
keep $m$ and $M$ separate throughout the implementation-facing notation.

A Merkle tree~\cite{merkle1988digital} is built over the ordered leaf array
$\KeyArray[0],\ldots,\KeyArray[M-1]$. An occupied leaf position $\ell$ stores
the Ring-LWE public key $pk_\ell \in R_q^N$, where $N$ is the Ring-LWE
key/witness dimension ($N=4$ in our implementation). Empty leaves store a
fixed null public key $\NullPK$. Each leaf key is hashed to a 256-bit digest
$h_\ell = \text{SHA256}(pk_\ell)$, and each internal node stores the SHA-256
hash of its two children's digests. Internal nodes are fixed-length 256-bit
strings, not polynomials. The 256-bit root $r$ commits to the full leaf-key
array.

A Merkle witness $\pi_\ell$ for leaf position $\ell$ consists of $L=\log_2 M$
sibling hashes along the path from leaf $\ell$ to the root, sufficient to
verify $h_\ell$ against $r$ in $O(\log M)$ hash operations. This logarithmic
path length is the mechanism underlying sublinear communication in laconic PSI.
When $M=\Theta(m)$, this is also $O(\log m)$.

\paragraph{Memory-efficient storage}
A na\"{i}ve implementation stores one array per tree level. Our implementation
uses a breadth-first array layout, storing all $2M-1$ nodes in a single array
of length $2M$, reducing tree storage to $O(M)$.


\subsection{Laconic Encryption}
\label{subsec:laconic_enc}

Laconic encryption~\cite{dottling2023laconic} is the core primitive enabling
sublinear-communication PSI.

\begin{definition}[Laconic Encryption]
A laconic encryption scheme
$\mathsf{LE} = (\mathsf{Setup}, \mathsf{KeyGen}, \mathsf{Upd},
\laconicenc, \laconicdec)$ is defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, M) \to \mathit{pp}$: generates public
        parameters for a leaf space of size $M$.
  \item $\keygen(\mathit{pp}) \to (pk, sk)$: generates a Ring-LWE key pair.
  \item $\upd(d, \ell, pk_\ell) \to d'$: updates digest $d$ at leaf position
        $\ell$ with public key $pk_\ell$.
  \item $\laconicenc(\mathit{pp}, d, \ell, \mathit{msg}) \to \mathit{ct}$:
        encrypts message $\mathit{msg}$ for leaf position $\ell$ using only
        the digest~$d$.
  \item $\laconicdec(sk_\ell, \ell, \mathit{ct}, w_\ell) \to \mathit{msg}$
        or $\bot$: decrypts using secret key $sk_\ell$ and server-side
        witness $w_\ell$ computed via $\witgen$.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Correctness:}
$\laconicdec(sk_\ell, \ell, \laconicenc(\mathit{pp}, d, \ell',
\mathit{msg}), w_\ell) = \mathit{msg}$ if and only if $\ell=\ell'$.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{The \laconicenc{} function and digest structure}
The digest is $d = r$, the Merkle root, a \emph{constant-size} commitment
to the server's public-key leaf array. The client does \emph{not} receive
$\{pk_\ell\}$; it encrypts solely using $r$ and the public parameters
$\mathit{pp}$. Formally,
$\laconicenc(\mathit{pp}, r, \ell, \mathit{msg}) \to
(c_0, c_1, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{d})$ takes the Merkle root $r$, a target leaf
position $\ell \in \{0,\ldots,M-1\}$, and a message. In the abstract scheme,
$\mathit{msg} \in \{0,1\}^L$; in our PSI protocol, the message is a
$\lambda$-bit nonce $r_j$ encoded into the ring element format required by the
scheme. The Ring-LWE ciphertext embeds the authentication-path structure
through the layered public-parameter matrix; the client does not require
sibling hashes separately, as the path is implicitly addressed by the circuit
structure of the encryption. Decryption uses
$w_\ell = \witgen(\text{full\_tree}, \ell)$, a Ring-LWE witness computed by
the \emph{server} from its privately held in-memory tree. Decryption succeeds
precisely when the server leaf position equals a leaf targeted by the client's
ciphertext.

In the 2-choice Cuckoo mode (Section~\ref{subsec:collision}), each element
has two candidate leaves $\ell_1 = H_1(x) \bmod M$ and
$\ell_2 = H_2(x) \bmod M$. The server stores each deduplicated record at one
assigned candidate leaf and records this association in a leaf-to-secret-key
map $\LeafMap$. The client encrypts its query nonce to both candidate leaves.
Decryption succeeds precisely when the server-assigned leaf for a matching
record equals one of the two candidate leaves for the client element.

\section{Protocol Description}
\label{sec:protocol}
% =============================================================================

Laconic PSI applies laconic encryption to PSI. In the implementation-facing
presentation below, $m=|S|$ is the number of server records, $M=2^L$ is the
Cuckoo/Merkle leaf-space size, and $\ell$ denotes a Merkle leaf position. This
separation is important because, under Cuckoo placement, a record index $i$
and its assigned leaf position $\ell$ need not be equal.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Laconic PSI: Offline Phase (Server)}
\label{alg:offline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Server dataset $S = \{s_1, \ldots, s_m\}$; leaf-space size $M=2^L$;
         security parameter $1^\lambda$
\ENSURE Public digest $d = r$; server retains full tree and $\LeafMap$
\STATE $\mathit{pp} \leftarrow \mathsf{Setup}(1^\lambda, M)$
       \COMMENT{Public parameters; published alongside digest}
\STATE Initialise $\KeyArray[0..M-1] \leftarrow \NullPK$ and $\LeafMap \leftarrow \emptyset$
\FOR{$i = 1$ to $m$}
  \STATE $h_i \leftarrow H(s_i)$ \COMMENT{SHA-256 of each record}
  \STATE $(pk_i, sk_i) \leftarrow \keygen(\mathit{pp})$
         \COMMENT{Ring-LWE key pair for this record}
  \STATE $\ell_{i,1} \leftarrow H_1(h_i) \bmod M$;
         $\ell_{i,2} \leftarrow H_2(h_i) \bmod M$
         \COMMENT{Two candidate leaves}
  \STATE $\ell_i \leftarrow \mathsf{CuckooInsert}(\KeyArray, pk_i,
         \ell_{i,1}, \ell_{i,2})$
  \STATE $\LeafMap[\ell_i] \leftarrow (i, sk_i)$
         \COMMENT{Record index and secret key stored at assigned leaf}
\ENDFOR
\STATE Build Merkle tree over $\KeyArray$; let $r$ be the root
\STATE \textbf{Publish} $d \leftarrow r$
       \COMMENT{Constant-size digest; NOT $\{pk_\ell\}$}
\STATE \textbf{Store locally:} full tree and $\LeafMap$
\RETURN $d$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Laconic PSI: Online Phase (Standard Two-Party PSI)}
\label{alg:online}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Client dataset $C = \{c_1, \ldots, c_n\}$; server digest $d=r$;
         public parameters $\mathit{pp}$; server holds full tree and $\LeafMap$
\ENSURE Both parties learn $C \cap S$; server does not learn $C \setminus S$;
        client does not learn $S \setminus C$ (standard semi-honest PSI security)
\STATE \textbf{Client side}
\FOR{$j = 1$ to $n$}
  \STATE $h_j \leftarrow H(c_j)$ \COMMENT{Hash client element}
  \STATE $\ell_{j,1} \leftarrow H_1(h_j) \bmod M$ \COMMENT{First candidate leaf}
  \STATE $\ell_{j,2} \leftarrow H_2(h_j) \bmod M$ \COMMENT{Second candidate leaf}
  \STATE $r_j \xleftarrow{\$} \{0,1\}^\lambda$
         \COMMENT{Fresh random nonce; client keeps $r_j \mapsto c_j$ map}
  \STATE $\mathit{ct}_{j,1} \leftarrow \laconicenc(\mathit{pp}, r, \ell_{j,1}, r_j)$
  \STATE $\mathit{ct}_{j,2} \leftarrow \laconicenc(\mathit{pp}, r, \ell_{j,2}, r_j)$
         \COMMENT{Encrypt nonce $r_j$ to both candidate leaves}
\ENDFOR
\STATE Transmit $\{\mathit{ct}_{j,c}\}_{j=1,c=1}^{n,2}$ to Server
       \COMMENT{Server sees only ciphertexts; element values are hidden}
\STATE \textbf{Server side}
\STATE \textit{Process at most $W$ occupied leaves concurrently via semaphore batching.}
\FOR{each occupied leaf $\ell \in \operatorname{dom}(\LeafMap)$}
  \STATE $(i, sk_\ell) \leftarrow \LeafMap[\ell]$
  \STATE $w_\ell \leftarrow \witgen(\text{full\_tree}, \ell)$
         \COMMENT{Ring-LWE witness for the assigned leaf position}
  \FOR{$j = 1$ to $n$}
    \FOR{$c \in \{1, 2\}$}
      \STATE $v \leftarrow \laconicdec(sk_\ell, \ell, \mathit{ct}_{j,c}, w_\ell)$
      \IF{$v \neq \bot$}
        \STATE Append $(i, j, v)$ to $\text{match\_list}$
               \COMMENT{Server gets nonce $v=r_j$ but not $c_j$}
      \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE Free $w_\ell$; trigger GC
\ENDFOR
\STATE Transmit $\text{match\_list}=\{(i,j,v)\}$ to Client
\STATE \textbf{Client side result}
\FOR{each $(i,j,v)$ in $\text{match\_list}$}
  \IF{$v = r_j$}
    \STATE Record $c_j \in C \cap S$
           \COMMENT{Client verifies nonce; identifies matching element}
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\FloatBarrier

\paragraph{Privacy model}
This protocol achieves \emph{standard semi-honest PSI} security: both parties
learn $C \cap S$; neither party learns non-matching elements of the other.
The server observes decryption success or $\bot$ for each occupied leaf and
client ciphertext pair, revealing which stored server record belongs to the
intersection; this is standard for server-aided PSI. However, the server does
\emph{not} learn $c_j$ for matching elements; it receives only a random nonce
$r_j$, and recovering $c_j$ from the candidate leaf positions requires
inverting $H$ under the fixed hashing model. The stronger one-sided model
(where the server learns only $|C|$, not the intersection elements) requires
an additional output-blinding OT round and is left for future work. For the
sanctions-screening use case, both parties learning the intersection is the
operationally correct model: the screener must identify flagged entities.

\paragraph{Communication analysis}
Each laconic ciphertext $\mathit{ct}_j$ comprises $(L+1)$ ring elements,
one per Merkle tree level, where $L=\log_2 M$ grows logarithmically with the
leaf-space size $M$. Each ring element is a degree-$D$ polynomial over
$\mathbb{Z}_q$; with bit-level packing (58~bits $\approx 7.25$~bytes per
coefficient):
\[
  |\mathit{ct}_j|
    = (L+1) \times D \times \frac{\log_2 q}{8}\,\text{ bytes.}
\]
Note that $N=4$ (the Ring-LWE key/witness dimension) does \emph{not} appear
here; the ciphertext consists of one polynomial per level, not $N$
polynomials. With 2-choice Cuckoo assignment
(Section~\ref{subsec:collision}), each client query is encrypted to both
candidate leaves, producing two laconic ciphertexts per query. Therefore, the
correctness-preserving implementation uses
\[
  2n \cdot |\mathit{ct}|
\]
client-to-server communication. This preserves the asymptotic
$O(n\log M)$ communication profile; when $M=\Theta(m)$, this is
$O(n\log m)$, with a factor-2 constant overhead from Cuckoo mode. For
$n=100$ at $m=10{,}000$ with $M=2^{14}$ ($L=14$):
$2 \times 100 \times 27.7$\,KB\,$=5.54$\,MB (vs. KKRT's $O(n+m)$ bandwidth
of $\sim$200\,MB). This logarithmic dependence is precisely the laconic
advantage.

\paragraph{Security}
Under the Ring-LWE assumption (Definition~1), the scheme achieves
semi-honest (honest-but-curious) security as proved in
D\"{o}ttling et al.~\cite{dottling2023laconic}: both parties learn
$C \cap S$; the server does not learn $C \setminus S$ and the client does
not learn $S \setminus C$. Extension to malicious security via
zero-knowledge proofs is left for future work.

\subsection{The Memory Bottleneck and Batching Solution}

\paragraph{Why memory constrains the evaluation}
Each Ring-LWE witness for one server record expands a ciphertext matrix
across all $L+1$ Merkle tree levels via GInvMNTT gadget decomposition. The
dominant memory consumer is this expanded gadget witness matrix:
\[
  M_{\text{witness}}
    \approx (L+1) \times N \times M_{\mathit{gad}} \times D \times 8\,\text{bytes,}
\]
where $M_{\mathit{gad}} = N \times \lceil\log_2 q\rceil = 4 \times 58 = 232$
is the gadget matrix width, $L = \log_2 M$, and $N=4$.
For the $m=10{,}000$ benchmark we use $M=2^{14}$ leaves, so $L+1=15$:
\[
  M_{\text{witness}} \approx 15 \times 4 \times 232 \times 256 \times 8
    \approx 28.5\,\text{MB (witness only).}
\]
Including NTT scratch buffers, Go runtime overhead, and tree state,
the measured per-worker working set is $M_{\text{worker}} \approx 85$\,MB (from
$\text{Peak RSS} / W = 6.5\,\text{GB} / 77 \approx 84$\,MB).
Processing all $m = 10{,}000$ records simultaneously would therefore require
$10{,}000 \times 85$\,MB $\approx 830$\,GB, far exceeding available HPC RAM.

\paragraph{Standard bounded-concurrency solution}
To enable large-scale evaluation we apply a counting-semaphore pattern
(standard in Go HPC workloads): at most $W$ goroutines hold live witnesses
concurrently, with explicit \texttt{runtime.GC()} calls to reclaim each per-worker
witness working set before the next task is loaded.

\begin{remark}[Peak worker memory]
\label{thm:batch}
With a $W$-slot counting semaphore, at most $W$ live witnesses exist
simultaneously. The witness matrix alone is bounded by
$W \times M_{\text{witness}}$, while measured peak RSS is better modelled as
\[
  S_{\text{peak}} \approx W \times M_{\text{worker}},
\]
where $M_{\text{worker}}$ includes the witness matrix, NTT scratch buffers,
tree references, and Go runtime overhead. This bound is independent of $m$
provided each worker releases its witness before the next task acquires the
semaphore.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Batching implementation}
We partition the $m$ server records into batches of size $B_s = 500$ and
client queries into batches of $B_c = 100$. Within each batch, $W$ goroutines
process server records concurrently, controlled by a counting semaphore.
The key loop structure is:
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE Allocate semaphore $\mathit{sem} \leftarrow \text{make}(\text{chan}, W)$
\FOR{each occupied leaf $\ell \in \operatorname{dom}(\LeafMap)$}
  \STATE $\mathit{sem} \leftarrow \text{acquire}$
         \COMMENT{block if $W$ workers are busy}
  \STATE \textbf{go} \{
  \STATE \quad $(i, sk_\ell) \leftarrow \LeafMap[\ell]$
  \STATE \quad $w \leftarrow \witgen(\mathit{tree}, \ell)$
         \COMMENT{allocates one per-worker witness working set}
  \STATE \quad \textbf{for} $j = 1$ to $n$ \textbf{do}
         \textbf{for} $c \in \{1,2\}$ \textbf{do} $\laconicdec(sk_\ell, \ell, ct_{j,c}, w)$
  \STATE \quad $\mathit{sem} \leftarrow \text{release}$; $\text{GC}()$
         \COMMENT{releases the working set before next goroutine enters}
  \STATE \}
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
This ensures that exactly $W$ witnesses reside in RAM at any time.

\subsection{Parallelisation}

The worker count is determined by the following adaptive formula:
\[
  W = \min\!\left(\lfloor \text{CPUs} \times 0.8 \rfloor,\;
  \left\lfloor \frac{\text{Available RAM}}{M_{\text{worker}}} \right\rfloor\right).
\]
We estimate $M_{\text{worker}}$ empirically from a calibration run and reserve 20\% of CPU cores for Go runtime garbage collection and memory
management. On our 96-core, 256\,GB evaluation node, this yields $W = 77$
workers.

\subsection{Distributed Architecture}

For time-sensitive deployments, we partition the server dataset across $K$
nodes, each processing a disjoint shard $S_k \subset S$ of size $m/K$. Each
shard builds an independent Merkle tree over a shard leaf space $M_k$ and
publishes its own digest. Consequently, the client encrypts each query for
each shard; in the 2-choice Cuckoo mode used in our evaluation, the client
upload is $O(2K n \log M_k)$, plus a small coordination message. When
$M_k=\Theta(m/K)$, this is $O(2K n \log(m/K))$. This preserves logarithmic
dependence on each shard size for fixed $K$, but the constant factor grows
linearly with the number of shards.

\subsection{Throughput Analysis}

One \emph{operation} (op) corresponds to processing one server record against
the full client ciphertext set: generating its Merkle witness, applying
GInvMNTT gadget decomposition, and executing $2n$ Ring-LWE decryption checks in the 2-choice Cuckoo mode.
Throughput stabilises at approximately 1.0~ops/sec for $m \geq 1{,}000$,
indicating memory-bandwidth-bound performance.

Profiling (wall-clock phase timers at $m=10{,}000$; CPU \texttt{pprof} at
$M=100$) reveals the following breakdown:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Server initialisation ($\approx 29\%$):} KeyGen, tree build,
        tree load into RAM.
  \item \textbf{Per-record intersection ($\approx 69\%$):} on-the-fly
        WitGen + \texttt{Dec} per server record.
  \item \textbf{Lattigo NTT ring operations ($\approx 28\%$ of CPU samples):}
        comprising \texttt{ring.BRed}~(6\%), \texttt{ring.MulCoeffsVec}~(10\%),
        \texttt{ring.NTTLazy}~(4\%), \texttt{matrix.GInvMNTT}~(2\%).
  \item \textbf{Memory/GC overhead ($\approx 25\%$) and goroutine sync
        ($\approx 24\%$):} inherent to the bounded-concurrency pattern.
  \item \textbf{Client encryption ($\approx 1\%$):} fast; not a bottleneck.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Per-shard digest architecture}
Each of the $K$ shards operates as an independent laconic PSI instance:
shard~$k$ holds a disjoint partition $S_k \subset S$ with $|S_k| = m/K$,
builds its own Merkle tree over leaf space $M_k$ with root digest~$r_k$, and
generates its own Ring-LWE public parameters. The coordinator broadcasts the
client's ciphertext batches to all shards.
Each shard independently runs the laconic decryption/intersection phase and
returns match records. The coordinator takes the union of per-shard matches.

In the distributed setting, each shard maintains an independent Merkle-root
digest. A client therefore encrypts each query once per shard, and in the
2-choice Cuckoo mode twice per shard. The total client-to-server upload is
$O(2K n \log M_k)$ for $K$ shards, or $O(2K n \log(m/K))$ when $M_k=\Theta(m/K)$. For fixed~$K$, this remains logarithmic
in the per-shard server size, but the constant factor grows linearly with
the number of shards. In our $K=7$ deployment with $n=100$ and $m=10{,}000$,
the Cuckoo-mode client upload is approximately $7 \times 5.54$\,MB
$\approx 38.8$\,MB, still far below KKRT's $O(n+m)$ baseline.

protocol. We include this comparison to make explicit the computational cost
accepted in exchange for post-quantum security and a reusable server digest.
The hardware contexts differ (Intel Xeon vs.\ AMD EPYC~7763) and we do not
re-run KKRT on our machine.

\paragraph{ALOS22~\cite{alos2022}}
ALOS22 published benchmarks on an Intel Core i7-7820X at 3.6\,GHz over a
10\,Gbps LAN at $m = 4{,}096$, $n = 256$, achieving a server runtime of
$\approx 19$~min. At our benchmark point ($m=10{,}000$, $n=100$), ALOS22's
$O(nm)$ server complexity extrapolates to:
\[
  19 \times \frac{10{,}000}{4{,}096} \times \frac{100}{256} \approx 18\,\text{min.}
\]
Our protocol requires 153~min at the same $(m, n)$ point, representing a $\approx 8\times$
overhead accepted in exchange for post-quantum security and a reusable digest
unavailable in the classical pairing-based setting. For a direct comparison at
ALOS22's own published point: at $m=4{,}096$, $n=100$, our protocol takes
$\approx 67$~min (interpolated from $m=2{,}000$: 33.7~min and
$m=5{,}000$: 83.5~min) vs.\ ALOS22's 19~min at $n=256$ on weaker
hardware; this suggests an approximate $5$--$10\times$ overhead under
non-identical hardware and workload assumptions.

\paragraph{HE-PSI~\cite{chen2017fast}}
Chen et al.\ report approximately 36~seconds online for $N_y = 5{,}000$
queries against $N_x = 2^{24}$ server records on 16~CPU threads. This
workload ($n = 5{,}000$, $m = 2^{24}$) differs substantially from our
benchmark point ($n = 100$, $m = 10{,}000$); a direct runtime comparison
is therefore not meaningful. We include HE-PSI for context: it achieves
sublinear communication but is not laconic in the reusable-digest sense,
and its concrete post-quantum security depends on the chosen BFV
parameters.

\paragraph{Our protocol}
To our knowledge, ours is the first implemented system in this comparison
that combines laconic $O(n \log M)$ communication, a reusable server digest,
and a lattice-based security assumption.

\subsection{Digest Reusability and Dynamic Updates}
\label{subsec:dynamic}

A key practical advantage of the laconic digest $r$ is its reusability: once
computed, the server may serve multiple client rounds without re-running the
offline phase. For dynamic updates:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Insertion of $s_{m+1}$:} With 2-choice Cuckoo assignment
        (Section~\ref{subsec:collision}), insertion may trigger a bounded eviction
        chain. Therefore, dynamic updates require updating the affected leaf paths
        along the chain; in the worst case, several leaves and their $O(\log M)$
        path nodes may change.
  \item \textbf{Deletion of $s_i$:} Look up the assigned leaf $\ell$ for
        $s_i$, assign a null key at that leaf, remove $\ell$ from
        $\LeafMap$, and recompute the affected $O(\log M)$ path hashes.
  \item \textbf{Batch of $\Delta$ updates:} Apply all insertions and evictions
        to the tree, then recompute $r$ in a single $O(M)$ pass. Given the
        eviction cascades of Cuckoo hashing, batch updates are strictly
        preferable for daily sanctions-list refreshes.
\end{itemize}

After any update, the public digest $r$ changes and clients must request the
new $r$ before issuing queries, requiring only an $O(1)$ fresh-digest
exchange.

\subsection{When Is Laconic Ring-LWE PSI Worth It?}

Despite the runtime overhead, three deployment scenarios motivate this design
relative to classical alternatives.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Long-term data protection.}
        Financial records with long retention horizons motivate lattice-based PSI; a
        production deployment for this setting would require stronger parameters
        such as $D=2048$.
  \item \textbf{Highly unbalanced datasets.}
        For sufficiently large server sets, and especially beyond the Cuckoo-mode
        communication crossover, the protocol substantially reduces repeated
        client-to-server communication relative to $O(n+m)$ PSI.
  \item \textbf{Overnight batch processing.}
        Applications with relaxed latency requirements, such as daily compliance
        batches, can absorb higher computation when reusable digests and
        lattice-based assumptions are priorities.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Hash-to-Leaf Mapping and Collision Analysis}
\label{subsec:collision}

PSI correctness requires that equal server and client elements be routed to at
least one common Merkle leaf, while non-equal elements should not create
spurious decryptions except with negligible cryptographic failure probability.

\paragraph{2-choice Cuckoo leaf assignment}
A naive modulo mapping into a leaf space of size $M=2^L$ can create
collision-induced false negatives when two different server records occupy the
same leaf. The expected number of pairwise collisions under one-hash modulo
mapping is approximately
\[
  E_{\text{coll}} \approx \frac{m(m-1)}{2M},
\]
which is an expected count, not a probability. For $m=10{,}000$ and
$M=2^{14}$, this heuristic is on the order of $3.0\times 10^3$ collisions.
To avoid this failure mode, all benchmarks in this paper use the 2-choice
Cuckoo leaf-assignment protocol. The server attempts to insert each element
into one of two candidate leaves
$\ell_1 = H_1(s_i) \bmod M$ or $\ell_2 = H_2(s_i) \bmod M$; if both are
occupied, standard Cuckoo eviction is used. Because the client does not know
which candidate leaf the server selected, the client encrypts each query nonce
to \emph{both} candidate leaves, producing $2n$ ciphertexts instead of $n$.
The asymptotic communication remains $O(n\log M)$, or $O(n\log m)$ when
$M=\Theta(m)$, with a factor-2 constant overhead.

\paragraph{Cuckoo implementation parameters}
The implementation uses domain-separated SHA-256-derived hash functions for
$H$, $H_1$, and $H_2$, with independent per-run salts for the Cuckoo hash
functions. Empty leaves are filled with null public keys so that the
Merkle tree has a uniform structure. In the reported $m=10{,}000$ benchmark,
we use $M=2^{14}$ leaves ($L=14$). If Cuckoo insertion fails, the table is
rebuilt with fresh salts and the failed attempt is recorded by the benchmark
harness. The artifact records the leaf-space size, hash salts, eviction bound,
and any stash configuration used for each benchmark run, so that the placement
step is reproducible.

\paragraph{Performance impact}
Cuckoo mode doubles the number of client ciphertexts, but since witness
generation and witness construction are unchanged, the additional ciphertext
checks were not a dominant cost in the measured runs. We therefore report the
main results directly in Cuckoo mode rather than relying on modulo-mapping
projections. The comparison with earlier modulo-mapping runs is treated only
as a sanity check, since small changes in shard placement, cache behaviour,
and Go scheduling can dominate the added decryption work at this scale.

\paragraph{Functional validation}
We tested Cuckoo mode on random sets with $m \in \{50, 100, 500\}$ and
injected collisions (elements with identical $H_1$-candidate leaves). Across
50~trials per configuration, the 2-choice Cuckoo approach produced zero false
negatives and zero false positives in the tested configurations. Additionally,
the distributed benchmarks at $m \in \{1{,}000, 2{,}000, 4{,}000, 8{,}000,
10{,}000\}$ with $K=7$ shards all completed successfully under Cuckoo mode,
verifying correctness at the largest benchmark scale evaluated in this work.

\subsection{Implementation Validation}

Cryptographic security follows from the DKLLMR23 proof under the Ring-LWE
assumption; our tests validate implementation behaviour, not the underlying
hardness assumption.

\paragraph{Functional validation}
All Known Answer Tests (basic, empty intersection, full intersection, and
repeated encryption) passed across 50~trials. Across the Known Answer Tests
and randomized Cuckoo-mode validation runs, we observed no decryption
failures. We treat this as implementation validation rather than a proof of
negligible decryption-error probability; formal correctness follows from the
DKLLMR23 construction under its parameter assumptions, while a tighter
empirical noise-margin study is left for future work.

\paragraph{Timing behaviour}
Encryption timing variation: $< 0.5\%$. Decryption timing difference
(match vs.\ non-match): $3.33\%$.

\paragraph{Timing side-channel note}
The 3.33\% timing differential between match and non-match arises from
different branch paths in the correctness check. For compliance deployments
where side-channel resistance is required, we provide a
\texttt{PSI\_CONSTANT\_TIME=1} flag that pads all decryption operations to a
fixed worst-case duration measured during initialisation. This reduces the
differential to $< 0.1\%$ at a throughput cost of $< 4\%$ (measured). For
pure batch offline processing, the unfixed variant is acceptable.

\paragraph{Handling duplicates}
Our implementation follows standard set-PSI semantics: server and client
inputs are canonicalised and deduplicated before hashing. Duplicate records
therefore do not contribute multiplicity to the output. Extending the
implementation to multiset PSI would require occurrence-index encoding or
explicit duplicate-count handling, which we leave for future work.

\section{FLARE: Proof-of-Concept Sanctions Screening}
\label{sec:flare}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Motivation}

European banks face a structural compliance conflict: they must screen
customers against government-mandated sanctions lists (anti-money laundering
law) without disclosing customer identifiers to third-party screening
providers (GDPR, data-minimisation principle~\cite{gdpr2016}). Current
approaches fail on at least one axis: full-list downloads create recurring
large data transfers and expose query patterns; hash-based screening is
vulnerable to rainbow-table attacks; and
classical PSI rests on quantum-vulnerable cryptography. FLARE addresses all
three concerns as a proof-of-concept prototype using the 2-choice Cuckoo
leaf-assignment mode described in Section~\ref{subsec:collision} to avoid
hash-collision false negatives. A production deployment would additionally
require stronger Ring-LWE parameters ($D=2048$), malicious-security
extensions, and geographically distributed or bandwidth-constrained WAN
evaluation.

\subsection{System Design}

FLARE implements the following workflow.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The sanctions provider computes the server digest (offline phase) and
        publishes the public parameters and Merkle-root digest daily;
        per-leaf Ring-LWE keys and tree state remain server-side.
  \item The bank hashes customer records locally.
  \item The bank encrypts hashes using \laconicenc{} and transmits ciphertexts
        to the provider.
  \item The provider runs the intersection phase and returns binary match results.
\end{enumerate}
GDPR compliance is enforced by construction: only Ring-LWE ciphertexts
over locally hashed identifiers cross the network; raw PII never leaves
the bank's infrastructure.

\subsection{Validation}

We deployed FLARE against 10{,}000 simulated sanctions entries using the
2-choice Cuckoo mode throughout. Single-node screening latency of
153.1~minutes (measured, $D=256$, $m=10{,}000$) is acceptable for overnight
batch processing. A networked distributed deployment across $K=7$ GCE nodes
achieves Cuckoo-mode screening in 12.7~minutes (measured). Linearly
extrapolating from this measured 10{,}000-entry cloud-cluster run suggests
that 100{,}000-entry screening would fit within a standard overnight batch
window of 8~hours under similar cloud-cluster conditions. In repeated daily
screening, the reusable digest also amortizes the provider's offline phase:
for $T$ client rounds using the same published digest, the per-round digest
preparation cost is $C_{\mathrm{offline}}/T$, while each client round retains
the measured online communication and intersection costs.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Prototype limitations}
Our implementation targets the semi-honest adversarial model. Extension to
malicious security would require zero-knowledge proofs of correct ciphertext
structure; we estimate this would add $O(n)$ ZK-proof overhead. We leave this
for future work.

\paragraph{Scale limitations}
All benchmarks are at $m \leq 10{,}000$ ($D=256$), which is the practical
limit of a single HPC node for the parameters used in this work. Scaling to
$m = 10^6$ is the most critical open question; the $O(n \log m)$
communication advantage grows with $m$, but a simple linear extrapolation
from the measured single-node slope projects $\sim$255~hours at $m=10^6$.
The distributed architecture mitigates this trend; a simple sharding
extrapolation suggests that larger shard counts could bring $m=10^6$ into a
daily batch window, but this remains to be validated.

\paragraph{Network evaluation}
Our distributed experiments were executed on a networked $K=7$ Google Compute
Engine cluster, so the reported sharded timings include cloud-cluster
communication and coordination overhead. However, all nodes were evaluated in
a controlled cloud-cluster setting rather than across geographically separated
or bandwidth-constrained WAN links. The communication crossover shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:comm_scaling} is a model-based communication estimate,
while our measured cloud-cluster experiments validate sharded execution up to
$m=10{,}000$. End-to-end WAN experiments under constrained links such as
1\,Gbps or 100\,Mbps remain future work.

\paragraph{GPU and hardware acceleration}
The two primary bottlenecks, NTT ring operations ($\approx 28\%$ of CPU
samples) within the intersection phase ($\approx 69\%$ of wall-clock
time), are well-suited to GPU acceleration.
GPU and hardware acceleration for NTT-style polynomial arithmetic is a
natural optimisation target for larger ring dimensions such as $D \geq
1{,}024$. GPU-NTT acceleration is our highest-priority near-term
optimisation and would directly support the upgrade to stronger parameter
settings such as $D=2048$ at practical runtimes.

\paragraph{Security level}
All benchmarks in this work use $D=256$, providing approximately
140-bit classical security and approximately 70-bit quantum security under
the estimator assumptions in Section~\ref{subsec:params}. Upgrading to
stronger parameters such as $D=2048$, targeting NIST PQC Level~1-style
security margins, is future work. Preliminary internal measurements suggest
a substantial runtime increase at larger ring dimensions, but a complete
$D=2048$ evaluation is left for future work and would likely require
GPU-NTT acceleration and distributed sharding.

\paragraph{Future directions}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Stronger post-quantum parameters ($D=2048$):} Upgrading to
        parameters targeting NIST PQC Level~1-style security margins, enabled
        by GPU-NTT acceleration and distributed sharding.
  \item \textbf{GPU/hardware acceleration:} CUDA/HBM acceleration of NTT ring
        operations and server initialisation.
  \item \textbf{Malicious security:} Fiat-Shamir ZK proofs for ciphertext
        well-formedness.
  \item \textbf{Larger benchmarks:} Evaluation at $m \geq 10^5$ using the
        distributed architecture.
  \item \textbf{WAN evaluation:} End-to-end latency measurements over
        geographically distributed or bandwidth-constrained WAN topologies.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We presented the first end-to-end implementation and
empirical evaluation of the DKLLMR23 Ring-LWE-based laconic PSI protocol
under standard semi-honest security. We do not propose a new cryptographic
construction or security proof; the construction and semi-honest proof are
inherited from DKLLMR23~\cite{dottling2023laconic}. Our contribution is
systems-oriented: we instantiate the construction in Go/Lattigo, engineer
memory-bounded witness generation, measure concrete costs, evaluate
distributed sharding, and study a compliance-screening prototype.

Using a reduced-parameter Ring-LWE instantiation ($D=256$,
$\approx$140-bit classical / 70-bit quantum security), we demonstrated that
lattice-based laconic PSI is practically feasible and identified the
bottlenecks that must be addressed to scale toward stronger NIST
PQC Level~1-style parameter settings such as $D=2048$.

Our empirical characterisation establishes that server initialisation
($\approx 29\%$) and per-record intersection ($\approx 69\%$) dominate
wall-clock time, with Lattigo NTT ring operations ($\approx 28\%$ of CPU
samples) as the critical GPU acceleration target. Our distributed sharding
architecture achieves $\approx 12.1\times$ measured latency reduction at $K=7$ nodes
from the measured 153.1-minute single-node baseline ($D=256$, $m=10{,}000$),
providing a concrete engineering path to larger-scale deployments.

FLARE is not intended as a production compliance system in its current form.
Rather, it demonstrates that the computational and communication profile of
Ring-LWE laconic PSI is compatible with overnight batch-style compliance
screening in a measured cloud-cluster setting, provided stronger parameter
settings such as $D=2048$, malicious-security hardening, and geographically
distributed or bandwidth-constrained WAN evaluation are completed.
